Focal Structure Analysis

Focal structures are key sets of individuals who may be responsible for coordinating events, protests, or leading citizen engagement efforts on social media networks. Discovering focal structures that are able to promote online social campaigns is important but complex. Unlike influential individuals, focal structures can affect large-scale complex social processes. In our prior work, we applied a greedy algorithm and bi-level decomposition optimization solution to identify focal structures in social media networks. However, the outcomes lacked a contextual representation of the focal structures that affected interpretability. To address this limitation, we have developed the contextual focal structure analysis (CFSA) model that enhances the discovery and interpretability of the key coordinating units of the network (Alassad and Agarwal, 2022). The model provided context in terms of the content shared by the focal structures through their communication network. The model utilizes multiplex networks, where one layer is the user network based on communications and social network relations (such as mentions, replies, friends, followers, etc.) and the second layer is the content co-occurrence network (such as hashtags, topics, comments, etc.). The two layers have interconnections based on the user content relations. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first effort in identifying influential sets of individuals instead of a set of influential individuals and reveals their interest on social networks. Very little research is available that investigates identifying key malicious groups spreading misinformation in social networks. To mention a few, researchers measured central users based on their influence in the network, resources they are consuming, and the information users can spread based on their positions in social network (Richardson and Domingos, 2002; Kempe and Kleinberg, 2003; Borgatti, 2005; Leskovec, Mcglohon, et al., 2007; Agarwal et al., 2008, 2012; Chen and Wang, 2009; Li et al., 2018). Likewise, complex networks on social platforms have been investigated to show how users‚Äô power in social networks (Kivran-Swaine, Govindan and Naaman, 2011; Chua, 2014) is used to spread, pass, and block information from other users. However, these methods investigated groups of influential users in the network, where these studies did not discover influential sets of individuals or reveal the users‚Äô interests in form of contexts or the participation in different platforms.

We introduced focal structure analysis (FSA) for social networks in (≈ûen et al., 2016), where a greedy algorithm was developed to identify focal structures responsible for spreading fake news in social networks. We reported small influential sets of users responsible for influencing thousands of users on Facebook. In (Alassad, Agarwal and Hussain, 2019), we presented a bi-level centrality-modularity model to examine intensive groups of co-commenters spreading fake news in a YouTube channel. This was a significant advancement to the original FSA and hence was named FSA 2.0. Our model combined two well-known social network analysis methods viz., centrality methods and modularity method to bridge the shortcoming in traditional community detection methods used in graph theory. The resultant combination is a bi-level linear optimization problem to realize/observe the interactions between the user-level and network-level as presented in Figure 2. We explored hidden intensive groups and ranked them for further investigations. We found key information spreaders in a complex social network by using a bi-level decomposition optimization method in a YouTube channel spreading fake news about the South China Sea conflict where the authors monitored the impacts of suspending these key sets of spreaders from the network (Alassad, Hussain and Agarwal, 2019). In an extended study, we used computational social science techniques to identify coordinated cyber threats to smart cities networks (Alassad, Hussain and Agarwal, 2019). In this research, we identified intensive sets of aggressors, measured their power utilizing the deviant cyber flash mob detection method, and then analyzed the network‚Äôs changes when a focal structure was suspended from the network. We implemented a comprehensive decomposition optimization model for locating key sets of commenters spreading conspiracy theory in online social networks (Alassad, Hussain and Agarwal, 2021), as depicted in Figure 3. In addition, we combined advanced computational social science and graph theoretic techniques to reveal adversarial information operations in online social networks (Alassad, Spann and Agarwal, 2021). We studied computational social science techniques to identify coordinated cyber threats to smart city static infrastructure networks (Alassad et al., 2021)

Our newest advancement to focal structure analysis, i.e., contextual focal structure analysis approach (CFSA) leverages context to improve discovery and interpretability of focal structures in social networks. We use multiplex network formalism to implement the idea, where different network layers could be used to represent various contexts such as relations among individuals on different platforms (Twitter, blogs, Facebook, YouTube, etc.), content/information flow network (e.g., reply/mention/share network, hashtag network, topic network, link/URL graph, metadata inferred covert connections among entities). The network layers could have interconnections based on user-user and user-content relations. We extended our FSA model presented in Figure 2 by adopting the multiplex network formalism. The CFSA model is designed to consider nonlinear relationships of online users in social network, which can be conceptualized as different layers of the multiplex network as shown in Figure 4 below. We provide the technical intuition into the CFSA model. The model consists of three main components, viz., data collection, CFS sets discovery, and CFS sets validation and analysis, as presented in Figure 5.

Due to the complexities in the analysis of different social media platforms and the unpredictability in the users‚Äô behavior (where users have many accounts on different social media platforms), the CFSA model is helpful to implement and examine cross-platform focal structures. The model presented here would accept more layers to include cross-platform aspect of the problem domain, i.e., content shared on different social media / communication platforms could be added to the CFSA multiplex network formalism (i.e., expands ùê¥ matrix shown in Figure 4). Also, this is a systematic method to manage the added information about users, and expand the structure of the multiplex network to include the online users‚Äô participation from different social media platforms like Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, etc.

The outcomes from the CFSA model are summarized in three discoveries in any social networks to track misinformation campaigns and its actors as the campaign proceeds as follow:
1. Discovery of new activities between users.
2. Discovery of new activities between contexts on social networks (e.g., co-referenced content, videos, information actors/users/commenters, hashtags, platforms).
3. Discovery of new activities between users and contexts (e.g., key coordinating structures spanning multiple information campaigns).
