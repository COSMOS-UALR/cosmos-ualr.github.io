Characterizing dynamics of social/campaign process for better characterization of campaign phases

We will discuss our approach to characterizing a dynamic social/campaign process. We will first provide a theoretical framework to characterize a social campaign and describe various theoretical constructs. Then, we will discuss operationalization of these constructs (computationally, and for social media) to characterize the dynamics of a campaign. Connective Action Based Framework

We have developed a conceptual framework and computational method for determining when technology-equipped crowd networks act together under connective action. Establishing this theoretical framework will help develop predictive models to monitor an emerging connective action event and determine the type of measures needed to support or counter the action. We propose to evaluate the extent that actors leverage connective action techniques to orchestrate information campaigns.

The computational methods used in social network analysis have opened the way for new approaches to be used to understand the psychological and social processes that occur when users take part in online social movements or digital activism. These techniques can provide insights to the underlying mechanisms that drive the phenomenon we see during connective action. The personal action framing in connective action offers in-depth insights about the role of identity in online movements such as protests, demonstrations, and mis/dis information campaigns (e.g., leaderless vs collective). Individual micro-actions, such as likes, mentions, and retweets, can contribute to collective action by making social information visible to personal networks, thereby creating circumstances for conditional cooperation (Spann, et al., 2020). The methods developed in this research offer rigorous new complements to traditional qualitative methods that address some of the limitations or critiques of the qualitative survey or manual annotation approaches, such as it being time-consuming and labor-intensive to extract insights. The use of computational methods to study the social science theories presented here can help researchers identify the grievances, processes, and social dynamics emerging from this type of cyber-collective movement that often serve as catalysts for such events (Agarwal et al., 2012; 2014). Opportunities for computational analysis as a complement to existing survey methods offer in-depth insights about the role of identity in online movements. The use of computational methods to study connective action is an understudied area that can also provide valuable insights into the behavior and emergence of these movements.

We have developed a socio-computational framework to assess the formation of cyber-collective communities, analyze interactions between actors and identify how they influence their communities. The primary goal of this framework is to demonstrate how computational methods may be used to detect and analyze connective action properties of collective identity, network organization, and mobilization (see Figure 2). This is done by using natural language processing to show how collective identities converge in social networks over time, using social network analysis to demonstrate how structural cohesiveness and organization of connective action networks emerges over time, and how ordinary differential equations can be used to determine when mobilization for an information campaign has reached critical mass. This theoretical framework is guided by the relevant social science theories of social and collective identity, resource mobilization through affordances and interdependence (see Figure 3), diffusions of innovations, and collective action. This framework will evolve over time by adding relevant theories and constructs. Further, computational methodologies to operationalize the theoretical constructs will evolve as well by adding novel ways of assessing nuanced cyber behaviors.

Collective Identity

Studies on computer-mediated communication shows that collective identity is strengthened by the anonymity and immersion that occurs within online groups as demonstrated by a model known as Social Identity Model of Deindividuation Effects (SIDE; Postmes, Spears and Lea, 1998; Spears and Postmes, 2015; Odag et al, 2019, p. 281). This amplification of social identity, as mediated by digital interactions, can both heighten polarization and facilitate deviant behavior (Douglas & McGarty, 2002, as cited in Odag, et al, 2019, p. 281). As group members become increasingly aligned with “group norms and expectations” their own attributes are suppressed and individuals “behave according to how they believe other people from the same social category will behave” (Chan, 2010, p. 1315). One aspect of this research probes the reliability of using NLP methods to measure the collective identity that is formed from these deindividuation effects using an LDA unsupervised distributional model to find the topics of discussion in online communities. We will apply principal component analysis (PCA) of the topic distributions to measure the inter-topic similarity between the topics being discussed within the online communities of participants in cyber protests. This NLP method demonstrates how we use textual content to measure collective identity as it emerges. Our experiments have demonstrated that as online protests or information campaigns peak, the distance between measured topics begins to overlap as their behaviors and discussion topics become more cohesive (Spann, et al., 2022b).

Network Organization

The second concept in examining connective action analyzes the structural components of an information campaign network. The organizational components are identified by applying Girvan-Newman modularity and community detection on the network, while also evaluating centrality methods. Our experiments for the #stopthesteal information campaign have demonstrated that the structure of the network also becomes more cohesive as measured by Girvan-Newman modularity, as an event emerges (Spann, et al., 2022b). To measure the organizational component of the connective action network, we examine how the information is spread between nodes and communities, i.e., communication network. If the organizational logic follows a pattern of sequential diffusion and not mutual exchanges, the information flow should be rather one-directional than reciprocal (Theocharis et al., 2017). Organizational nature of a communication network can be measured by calculating the degree centrality of the network structures, where we measure the in-degree, out-degree, and betweenness centrality for each node and then aggregate for the entire network. This approach can be used to characterize the affordance-interdependence properties discussed in Figure 3. Communities with a low degree centralization suggest a more decentralized communication than hierarchical or organizational (Spann, et al., 2020). Our experiments for the #stopthesteal information campaign have demonstrated that the structure of the network also becomes more cohesive as measured by Girvan-Newman modularity, as an event emerges (Spann, et al., 2022b) as shown in Figure 4.

Figure 4. Modularity of the #stopthesteal network over time. Decreasing modularity during November 3 – 10, 2020 indicates an emergence of cohesiveness and collectivity in the network. In other words, the network can be imagined as ‘coming together’. Lower values of modularity indicate a more cohesive network. Higher values indicate a more fractured network.



Mobilization

The mobilization aspect leverages the diffusion of innovations theory (DOI) to show how users adopt into social media campaigns. The psychological and social behaviors that are understood from the DOI model can be applied to social networks to categorize social media users into adoption categories and to use diffusion curves to determine if a campaign has reached its tipping point (see Figure 5). We demonstrate this application of DOI theory to social networks in (Spann, et al., 2022a), where we show how the spread of misinformation follows a trajectory pattern similar to the adoption of new technologies. The framework presented here considers these two approaches to understanding the diffusion of information in a connective action campaign. This view can be used to help shape collective identity and understand whether a collective response is a function of aggregation effects or specific interaction conditions within a group. This research also considers how online social networks are used for the purpose of social media campaigns. The sociotechnical theories of affordance and interdependence amongst users are applied to understand how individual user participation impacts the rate of adoption into an information campaign. The interactions between users in a connective action campaign can provide insights to help determine how future actors will react as information propagates through the network. Leonardi distinguished between these types of actions by labeling them as individualized, shared, and collective affordances (Leonardi, 2013). The interdependencies created between users (see Figure 6) creates a production function that allows us to measure the diffusion of information in a cyber campaign. The initial observations from our analysis support a mathematical characterization for connective action campaigns and show how affordances can be used to identify the inter-user message strategy (Spann, et al., 2020).

s-Function Characteristics

We leverage the diffusion of innovations theory to examine the trajectory of the overall campaign as it evolves. The second derivative test is then applied to identify changes in the slope of the s-curve. The second derivative test is a method in multivariable calculus used to determine if a critical point of a function is a local minimum, maximum, or saddle point. This empirical approach provides an initial quantitative method to find the points at which the slope of the curves shifts from the accelerating stage to the point at which the campaign reaches maximum diffusion or critical mass, and when the slope flattens out or enters the decelerating stage. Since DOI theory has been applied to various disciplines, a key benefit to this approach is that the s-curves are platform independent. Understanding these processes provides a foundation for predicting when an information campaign reaches critical mass.

Detection and Impact Assessment (Operationalization)

To evaluate this framework, we will use our existing data repositories (and collect new) such as COVID-19 anti-lockdown protests on Twitter for Operation Gridlock and the armed protests that resulted in the shutdown of the Michigan State Capitol, protests from the Stop the Steal rallies resulting from the 2020 presidential election, and misinformation campaigns propagating COVID-19 misinformation. The Michigan protest data contained 16,383 tweets from 9,985 users mentioning #MichiganProtest, #MiLeg, #Endthelockdown, and #LetMiPeopleGo hashtags were collected over a 50-day period from April 01, 2020 to May 20, 2020 (see Figure 7). Twitter data was collected for the #stopthesteal protests from January 1, 2020 through January 31, 2021 resulting in 838,395 tweets (see Figure 4). Nine COVID-19 misinformation and public discourse hashtags are being used in our experiments and include a broad range of topics related to COVID-19 such as lockdowns, facemasks, and vaccines (see Figure 8). The topics were chosen around highly debated issues that may have some call to action consistent with connective action campaigns.

We offer an approach to understanding collective action theory by providing the scientific community with a new way to evaluate the concepts associated with connective action while generating results that are interpretable and useful across disciplines. Our research is grounded in the theoretical behavioral theories that are catalysts for social movements, while the computational techniques presented here provide novel, scalable, and extensible methods to understand collective identity, network organization, and mobilization in connective action movements. There have been limited computational studies examining the sociological and organizational concepts of connective action. A major impediment to quickly understanding the behaviors that drive these cyber-crowd/movements is that they often require interviews and surveys of the participants to extract detailed information about who is at the center of organizing these groups, what identities the group associates itself with, and how or why people were drawn in to participate. Computational analysis as a complement to existing survey methods offers in-depth insights about the role of identity in online movements. Some of these studies have taken an analytical approach to focus on individual properties of connective action and how it differs from collective action, but there is no research that brings all the concepts together under a computational social science approach. The research and framework presented here provide a strong foundation for this important area of emerging socio-technical behaviors. The case studies presented here have highlighted the social science and behavioral theories involved in understanding social identity and affordances in social networks, interdependence, and resource mobilization through critical mass theory. This work presents a theoretical framework for operationalizing connective action theory using computational methods grounded in social network analysis and provides. This interdisciplinary approach bridges social science theories with computational science techniques to advance the computational social science discipline. In the context of connective action, these computational methods offer a new way to quickly identify behaviors and attributes shared within a social movement, which provides an important step in advancing the understanding of the emergence of collective action on digital platforms.
